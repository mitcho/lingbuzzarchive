The Iconic-Symbolic Spectrum
Gabriel Greenberg
February 2021
 

The distinction between iconic and symbolic representation, originally due to C.S. Peirce, has found growing currency in linguistics and cognitive science, even while underlying division remains obscure. In approaching this problem, I am guided by Ferdinand de Saussure's famous dictum that linguistic signs bear a merely "arbitrary" relationship to the concepts they express, in contrast to any "natural" or "inner" connection. By reimagining Saussure's opposition through the lens of contemporary formal semantics, I hope to shed light on these divergent expressive strategies for encoding content in representational form. In iconic semantics, the relationship between sign and content is mediated by a natural dependency between the form of the sign and its content; whereas, in symbolic semantics, the relationship between sign and content is one of unmediated juxtaposition.
