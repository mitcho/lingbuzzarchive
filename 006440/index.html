Learning Subcategorization Properties of Attitude Verbs in Wh-in situ Languages
Nick Huang, Yu'an Yang, Valentine Hacquard, Jeffrey Lidz
January 2022
 

Cross-linguistically, attitude verbs like “think” and “know” can be distinguished by their subcategorization properties: “think” selects only declarative complement clauses, while “know” selects both declaratives and interrogatives. This subcategorization distinction, readily apparent in languages with overt wh-movement, is obscured in wh-in situ languages like Mandarin Chinese, where interrogative complement clauses are string-identical with declarative complements that happen to contain an in situ wh-phrase (or some other interrogative element). A study of four Mandarin child-ambient speech corpora confirms that these string-ambiguous complement clauses are common for both “think” and “know”-like verbs. At the same time, corpus results also show that there are robust non-word order cues – speech act and question particles – that can be very informative for learning subcategorization differences between these verbs and what scope an in situ wh-phrase might take. These results further indicate that such speech act-related information can play a useful role in the acquisition of attitude verb syntax.
