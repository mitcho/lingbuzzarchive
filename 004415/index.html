The derivation of verb initiality in Santiago Laxopa Zapotec
Jeff Adler, Steven Foley, Jed Pizarro-Guevara, Kelsey Sasaki, Maziar Toosarvandani
March 2018
 

Languages with verb-subject-object (VSO) word order are amongst the most common in the world, and yet they pose a fundamental mystery. If the subject is structurally superior to the object, there is no way it can appear in underlying structure between the verb and object. In languages with rigid VSO order, one prominent solution invokes head movement of the verb to a position on the left of the subject. For other languages, ones in which VSO alternates with VOS, phrasal movement is commonly thought to be responsible. Recent work by Clemens & Coon (to appear) on Mayan has argued that head movement can still give rise to alternating word order if the resulting VSO order is manipulated postsyntactically. We describe the inverse scenario. Santiago Laxopa Zapotec is, like other Zapotec languages, rigidly VSO. Nonetheless, we argue that it uses phrasal movement to derive its verb-initial word order, based on evidence from adverbs, copular clauses, and light verb constructions.
