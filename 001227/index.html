Patterns of Dative-Nominative Alternations
Artemis Alexiadou, Elena Anagnostopoulou, Christina Sevdali
March 2011
 

In this paper, we investigate Dat-Nom alternations in dialects of German and Dutch and compare the patterns attested in these languages to Ancient Greek (AG), Japanese and Icelandic. We argue that there are three main factors regulating variation within and across languages: (a) Whether the alternations are limited to ditransitives (Low and Upper German, Dutch, Japanese) or they are also found with monotransitives (Luxemburg German, AG, Icelandic). (b) Whether the alternations affect IOs in ditransitives and non-theme DOs of monotransitives (Luxemburg German, AG) or only DOs in ditransitives and monotransitives (Icelandic). (c) Whether the alternations happen in passives, i.e. constructions containing an implicit external argument (AG, Dutch, Japanese, German for a group of speakers) or in non-passives, i.e. constructions lacking an implicit external argument (Icelandic, German for another group of speakers). Factor (a) is attributed to the mixed status of dative: structural vs. inherent/ lexical. Factors (b) and (c) are argued to reduce to properties of the voice system, more specifically to properties of the heads Voice and v (in a decomposition approach along the lines of Alexiadou, Anagnostopoulou & Schaefer 2006).
