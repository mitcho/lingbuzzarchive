Polarity Sensitivity and Fragments in Irish
James McCloskey
December 2023
 

In Irish, negative polarity items may function, in apparent isolation, as fragment answers – a possibility which seems initially to be both theoretically and typologically anomalous. This paper explores some theoretical consequences of that fact. It begins by arguing (following Acquaviva 1996) that Irish entirely lacks the kind of inherently negative expressions which appear in Negative Concord systems. It possesses instead a rich inventory of NPI's. The possibility of fragment NPI's is linked with an independent property of the language -- namely, that it possesses a clause-bound movement operation, known as Narrative Fronting (Ó Siadhail (1989), McCloskey (1996)), in which a narrow scope indefinite may be raised to a position to the left of the verb in a finite negative clause. A crucial property of this fronting is that it does not expand the scope of the fronted indefinite. The proposed linking eliminates the apparent anomaly and (initial appearances notwithstanding) provides an argument in favour of the move-and-delete line of analysis for fragment answers advocated especially by Jason Merchant (2004). It also brings some non-trivial implications for the theory of recoverability for ellipsis.
