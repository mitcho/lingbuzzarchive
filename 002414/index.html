Negative Knowledge from Positive Evidence
Charles Yang
March 2015
 

Why can’t we say “the asleep cat”? There is a class of adjectives in English, all of which start with a schwa (e.g., afraid, alone, asleep, away etc.), that cannot be used attributively in a prenominal position. A frequently invoked strategy in language acquisition research is indirect negative evidence. For instance, if the learner consistently observes paraphrases such as “the cat that is asleep”, then the conspicuous absence of “the asleep cat” may be a clue for its ungrammaticality (Boyd & Goldberg 2011). In this article, we provide formal and quantitative evidence from child directed English data to show that such learning strategies are untenable. However, the child can rely on positive data to establish the distributional similarities between this apparently idiosyncratic class of adjectives and locative particles (e.g., here, over, out, etc.) and prepositional phrases. With the use of an independently motivated principle of gener- alization (Yang 2005), the ungrammaticality of attributive usage can be productively extended to the adjectives in questions.
