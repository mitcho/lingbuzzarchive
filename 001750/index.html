Radical defectivity: implications of Xhosa expletive constructions for Bantu Case and flavors of T and v
Vicki Carstens, Loyiso Mletshe
January 2014
 

This paper on Xhosa expletive constructions explores what happens when all the agreement is removed from a Bantu clause. The result is a type of sentence in which subjects surface low, object shift is precluded, and Case-licensing is accomplished by means of a non-canonical strategy involving a middle-field Focus projection. Aspects of the syntax of ECs provide striking evidence that full DPs in Xhosa require Case licensing. Yet Xhosa also exhibits some Case-anomalies that led Harford Perez 1985, Diercks 2012 to argue that abstract Case is lacking in Bantu, and Halpert 2011, 2012 to propose that full DPs in closely related Zulu have intrinsic Case. We conclude that the standard diagnostics for Case yield misleading results for Bantu, and provide some reanalyses of the problem facts. Augmentless negative polarity items in Xhosa have distributional properties which motivated Halpert 2011, 2012 to claim that in Zulu, NPIs alone have uCase features. We argue that the reverse is true: augmentless NPIs are the only nominals that do not require and cannot bear Case. This bars them from preverbal subject position. A special licensing requirement further restricts their distribution: they must move leftwards, as Haegeman (1995) argued for n-words in West Flemish. Since v* cannot shift objects, only edge features of Appl and Caus make leftward NPI-movement possible vP-internally.
