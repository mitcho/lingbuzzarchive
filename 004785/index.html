Detecting Contiguity-Prominence
Norvin Richards
September 2019
 

In Richards (2010, 2016) I developed a theory that was meant to predict which syntactic movements would be overt and which would be covert (named "Contiguity Theory" in Richards (2016)). I proposed universal conditions on the prosodic consequences of Probe-Goal relations, which are met in different ways in different languages because of independently observable prosodic parameters. On this account, a given language has obligatorily overt wh-movement, for example, not because of a strong wh-feature in C, but because of universal conditions on the prosody of wh-questions, interacting with language-specific properties of the prosody of the language in question. I claimed that languages could be distinguished by a binary prosodic parameter; languages were to have either "Left-active" or "Right-active" prosodic systems, tending to realize prosodic prominence at either the left or the right edges of phonological phrases. In this paper I offer new support for this binary parameter, describing experiments which reliably classify languages as either Left-active or Right-active, and show how the results of these experiments match the predictions of Contiguity Theory.
