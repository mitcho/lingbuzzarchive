Psycholinguistics and Phonology: the forgotten foundations of generative phonology
Naiyan Du, Karthik Durvasula
December 2023
 

Research over the last few decades has consistently questioned the sufficiency of abstract/discrete phonological representations based on putative misalignments betweenpredictions based on such representations and the observed phonetic measurements. Here, we first suggest that many of the arguments ride on a misunderstanding of some of the original claims within the generative phonology tradition, and in fact, the typical contradictory evidence furnished is consistent with those claims. We then narrow in on the phenomenon of incomplete neutralisation and show again that it is consistent with the classic generative view of abstract/discrete representations. We further point out that extant accounts of the phenomenon do not achieve important desiderata and don’t explain why there are actually at least two different kinds of incomplete neutralisation that don’t stem from task effects/confounds. Finally, we present new experimental data and our explanation that the phenomenon is an outcome of planning using abstract/discrete phonological knowledge.
