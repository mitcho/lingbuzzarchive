Free Merge, Agree, & Phase-Cancellation: An Account of (Anti-)That-Trace Effects
William Kruger
May 2017
 

This paper proposes an account of that-trace and anti-that-trace effects within a “Free Merge” framework where wellformedness is determined by conditions of the interfaces (Chomsky 2008, 2013, 2015, 2016; Boeckx 2010). The that-trace effect is a constraint where subjects cannot extract long-distance without deletion of the complementizer "that". The anti-that-trace effect is a lesser-known inverse of this constraint, where "that" cannot be deleted when the subject undergoes short-distance movement. The major proposal of this paper redefines Agree to capture both effects. Agree evaluates the output of Merge, and, like Label, Agree cannot “see” traces/lower copies. Therefore, if the subject extracts, it will be invisible, leaving ϕ-features of C (=that) unvalued. Following Chomsky (2015), an operation of “phase-cancellation” (=that-deletion) may apply, allowing Agree to check uϕ on C prior to movement. This is the that-trace effect. If the subject extracts short-distance, however, phase-cancellation is prohibited, since short subject movement would potentially violate antilocality. This is the anti-that-trace effect. In addition, I propose that if uϕ remain unvalued within a phase, Feature Inheritance is not required, and said features may be valued on C in the next phase. This is termed Delayed Agree, and I extend the account to capture so-called anti-agreement effects in Berber and other languages, where ϕ-agreement is disrupted in the context of short subject movement.
