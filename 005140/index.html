Discontinuous agreement in Semitic: A Fission-based approach
Matthew Hewett
April 2020
 

Discontinuous agreement---namely, agreement with a single argument which is expressed in more than one position on the verb---has received two broad treatments in the previous literature on the morphological operation Fission. The first approach reduces Fission to a special case of iterated Vocabulary Insertion applying just in case previous cycles incompletely matched/discharged the full set of features of a syntactic terminal (Noyer 1992; Halle 1997; Harbour 2008; Campbell 2012). The second approach, laid out in Arregi and Nevins (2012), posits a distinct Fission operation which occurs derivationally prior to Vocabulary Insertion, splitting up certain features and copying all others. I argue for the latter approach on the basis of a novel set of allomorphic alternations which demonstrate that Fission (and linearization) feed (and therefore cannot be equated with) Vocabulary Insertion. I argue that a detailed comparison of discontinuous agreement paradigms across Semitic languages provides strong evidence for a modular view of the postsyntactic component whose operations are constrained by higher order principles such as locality and featural markedness.
