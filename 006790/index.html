Tripartite/inactive case in Samoan nominalizations: On ergativity and unaccusativity
Jens Hopperdietzel, Artemis Alexiadou
August 2022
 

In Samoan nominalizations, the ergative/absolutive alignment in verbal clauses is replaced by a typologically rare tripartite/inactive alignment that distinguishes all three types of subjects: While unaccusative subjects receive inalienable possessive case, unergative subjects receive alienable possessive case. Transitive subjects however maintain their ergative case marking from the verbal domain (Mosel 1992). In this paper, we demonstrate that this exceptional case pattern follows naturally from four independent syntactic factors that combine in Samoan nominalizations: (i) split-possessive case marking that is sensitive to the syntactic position of (in)alienable possessors (Myler 2016, Alexiadou 2003), (ii) split-intransitivity where agentive and patientive subjects are merged in designated syntactic positions, (iii) syntactic ergativity that arises from the prepositional nature of transitive subjects (Polinsky 2016), and (iv) the unaccusativity restriction on nominalizations which requires the absence of DP-arguments in Spec, VoiceP under n (Bruening 2013, Alexiadou 2001). Our analysis of Samoan nominalizations therefore has further implications on recent debates about the source of syntactic ergativity (Tollan & Clemens 2022, Coon et al. 2014) and the status of external arguments in nominalizations (Burukina 2021, Imanishi 2020).
