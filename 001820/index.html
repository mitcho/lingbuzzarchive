Head Movement in Moro DPs: Evidence for a Unified Theory of Movement
Peter Jenks
June 2013
 

In Moro, a Kordofanian language spoken in the Nuba Mountains of Sudan, noun phrases are characterized by the order noun-demonstrative-numeral-adjective, but nouns also precede prepositions and can strand all of these modifiers in, e.g., passive constructions. This paper presents arguments that these instances of noun movement are instances of head movement, N-to-D within DP,proposed for Kiswahili by Carstens (1991), and then head movement of the noun around the preposition, and finally long head movement to subject position (cf. Lema & Rivero, 1990). I present several arguments for head movement, the simplest of which is that no syntactic material besides the noun can occur in this initial position. However, the latter two instances of movement clearly violate the Head Movement Constraint (Travis, 1984). I argue that the way through this paradox is to adopt a theory of head movement as movement to a specifier position (Matushansky, 2006).
