Grammar change – A case of Darwinian cognitive evolution
Hubert Haider
April 2020
 

This paper claims that grammar change is a case of Darwinian evolution that targets the cognitively encapsulated, procedural parts of grammar. The cognitively accessible, declarative content of grammars is open for social changes. The grammar of a language is a complex neuro-cognitively represented program. Such programs are results of an ongoing evolution, that is, results of the interplay between variation and selection, on the level of cognitive processes. In cognitive evolution, the same principles apply as in biological evolution, but the domains of biology and cognition are different, of course. Evolution of grammar is not a facet of biological evolution. It is a domain of evolution on its own. Since the same general abstract principles are at work, the structure of evolutionary processes is parallel in each domain. As a consequence, many theoretical insights of a century of research in population genetics can be cautiously adduced and applied to the explanation of grammar change.
