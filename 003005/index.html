Non-monotonic derivations
Fabian Heck
May 2016
 

Among the most prominent principles within derivational theories of syntax are the Strict Cycle Condition (SCC) and the Minimal Link Condition (MLC). Despite the fact that these two principles have proved useful in many cases, it has been noted in the literature that some syntactic analyses, well motivated otherwise, are in conflict with either the MLC or the SCC. In this manuscript, I make a new proposal as to how one may reconcile SCC and MLC with these analyses while at the same time maintaining SCC and MLC in a maximally strong form. To this end, I propose that the (often implicit) assumption that the objects generated by syntactic derivations form sequences of monotonically growing trees should be abandoned. Rather, I suggest that syntactic trees may shrink temporarily. I call such derivations "non-monotonic," and I illustrate that they have a series of welcome side effects. Non-monotonic derivations derive equidistance effects, account for Holmberg's Generalization and for certain inversion phenomena; they provide a natural explanation for certain anti-Principle C effects thereby making concepts such as chain formation or lethal ambiguity superfluous. And they may offer a handle for explaining the absence of superiority effects and certain islands.
