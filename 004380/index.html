That's all
Vincent Homer
December 2018
 

The claim I make is that copular sentences give us a direct access to the core meaning of 'all'. In such environments, 'all' can give rise to a smallness effect: (1) a. All (that) John ate for lunch was a banana. b. All (that) John ate for lunch was a roasted pig. A fairly good paraphrase of (1a) is 'the only thing that John ate for lunch was a banana'. (1a) and (1b) convey that what John ate for lunch wasnâ€™t much, which, in the case of (1b), sounds funny. I show that the smallness effect, together with two other characteristic inferences, can be derived from a superlative semantics for 'all'.
