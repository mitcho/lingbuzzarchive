Relative Truth and the First Person
Friederike Moltmann
February 2010
 

In recent work on context-dependency, it has been argued that certain types of sentences give rise to a notion of relative truth. In particular, sentences containing predicates of personal taste and moral or aesthetic evaluation as well as epistemic modals are held to express a proposition which is true or false only relative to a standard of taste or knowledge or an agent. This paper argues that the sentences that apparently give rise to relative truth express what I will call ‘first-person-based genericity’, a form of generalization that is based on an essential first-person application of the predicate. The account differs from the standard relative-truth account in crucial respects: it is not the truth of the proposition expressed that is relative to the first person; the proposition expressed by a sentence with a predicate of taste rather has absolute truth conditions. Instead it is the propositional content itself that requires a first-personal cognitive access whenever it is entertained. This account avoids a range of problems that standard relative-truth theories of the sentences in question face and explains a number of further peculiarities that such sentences display.
