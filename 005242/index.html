Super Linguistics: an Introduction
Pritty Patel-Grosz, Salvador Mascarenhas, Emmanuel Chemla, Philippe Schlenker
April 2022
 

We argue that formal linguistic theory, properly extended, can provide a unifying framework for diverse phenomena beyond traditional linguistic objects. We summarize key achievements of syntax, semantics and pragmatics, pertaining to constraints on possible structures and meanings, the role of variables, and the typology of linguistic inferences. We then show how syntax and semantics can help illuminate diverse non-standard objects. First, we discuss structure and meaning in animal communication, beginning with birdsong, which has been analyzed as exhibiting sophisticated finite-state grammars. This is followed by a discussion of the semantics of primate calls â€“ where we state precise hypotheses about their lexical semantics, argue that no non-trivial rules of call composition are currently needed, and suggest that rules of competition among calls might play an important pragmatic role. Turning to human language, new developments make it possible to ask how iconicity interacts with logical semantics in spoken language, in sign language and in gestures. Going beyond language, syntax and semantics have recently been extended further, to pictorial meaning, to the syntax and semantics of visual narratives, and even to the syntax and semantics of music and dance, using a mix of new methods and standard tools of formal linguistic theory (e.g. variables). Finally, semantics is increasingly used to illuminate constraints on logical and non-logical concepts in the 'language of thought', leading to new accounts of reasoning. The aim of this overview is to show how the application of formal linguistic methodology (and methodology inspired by formal linguistics) to non-standard objects yields non-trivial insights, thus opening up a promising line of inquiry.
