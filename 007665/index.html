The unploughed field: speculations on a formal approach to charted and uncharted territory
Nigel Duffield
October 2023
 

This is a final draft (of the Proceedings version of) a plenary talk to be delivered in December 2023 at a conference on Systemic Functional Linguistics, in Hanoi. As the abstract suggests, it aims at a rapprochement between generativist and functionalist approaches to Vietnamese phrase-structure. In all likelihood, this talk will please no one: functionalists are unlikely to be persuaded by the need for any of the formal principles introduced here, while generativists may be equally averse to a theory that runs "from intention to articulation" (Levelt 1989), that treats LF as a universal, pre-syntactic, level of representation - the input to syntax - and which draws greater inspiration from Slobin's (2000, 2003) "Thinking for Speaking" hypothesis than from recent advances in generative theory (post 2000). Nevertheless, there may be some non-aligned linguists for whom this paper is of interest. "In this paper, I try to make a case for understanding Vietnamese phrase structure in terms of universal syntactic principles, introducing the framework of generative typology. This approach can be viewed as offering a rapprochement between conventional formalist vs functionalist research paradigms: whilst adopting several core mechanisms of mainstream generativism, including especially a syntactic construal of scope relations (“c-command”), the approach nevertheless shares with functionalist approaches the idea that underlying phrase structure is largely shaped by the meaning that speakers intend to convey. This thought experiment uses examples drawn from a variety of typologically distinct languages, with a focus on Predicate-Initial (‘VSO’) languages, Modern Irish, in particular."
