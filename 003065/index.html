The universal structure of categories. Towards a formal typology
Martina Wiltschko
July 2014
 

A new theory of grammatical categories, the Universal Spine Hypothesis, is proposed which reinforces generative notions of Universal Grammar while accommodating insights from linguistic typology. The essence of this new theory is that language-specific categories are constructed from a small set of universal categories and language-specific units of language.  The set of universal categories is hierarchically organized with each layer defined by a unique function in a manner reminiscent of cartographic approaches, but without being ‘substance-based’ (e.g., it is fundamentally formal rather than relying on meaning).   Throughout the book the Universal Spine Hypothesis is compared to two alternative theories: the Universal Base Hypothesis (essentially the traditional generative view) and the No Base Hypothesis (essentially the sceptical typologist’s position). Based on a variety of languages (including Blackfoot, Halkomelem, and Upper Austrian German) and based on a variety of categories and constructions (including tense, aspect, subjunctive, case, and demonstratives) the monograph attempts to reconcile these contradictory linguistic traditions.
