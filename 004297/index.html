Learning lexical classes from variable phonology
Stephanie Shih
March 2018
 

This paper presents a computational approach that induces the relevant lexical classes for morphologically-conditioned phonology from different phonological patterns on the surface, without a priori specifications of lexical class. The approach is an unsupervised dual-stage model using bottom-up lexical class discovery and grammar-based hypothesis testing. The current study revisits the lexical class-conditioned phonological behaviors of the supposed categorical distinction between English content and function classes. The classes learned by the computational model are shown herein to be quantitatively better at capturing morphologically-conditioned phonology, as compared to classes assumed by long-standing, top-down approaches. Given the results, the paper argues that utilizing the dual-stage model with feature discovery and selection within a probabilistic grammar framework allows learners to efficiently hone in on the most robust generalizations of lexical classifications over natural language evidence.
