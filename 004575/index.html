Nominalizations. The Case of Nominalizations of Modal Predicates
Friederike Moltmann
February 2020
 

Nominalizations have received considerable interest in linguistics, and some nominalizations have also received particular interest in philosophy, namely those that stand for ‘philosophical entities’ (to use Montague’s 1969 term), for example events, tropes, facts, and modal objects. The paper will focus on such nominalizations and give a case study of nominalizations of modal predicates, which have received little, if any, attention in the literature so far, nominalizations of the sort 'need', 'possibility', 'permission', 'obligation', and 'ability'. It will argue that nominalizations of modal predicates require recognizing a novel ontological category of modal objects, entities that are closely related to attitudinal objects (beliefs, claims, decisions, requests etc) and arguably are central for the semantics of modal sentences themselves. The paper discusses three semantic nominalization strategies by which modal objects can be introduced.
