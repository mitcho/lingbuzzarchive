East is East? Translated and Original Letters in the Embassy Books of Ivan III [Восток есть Восток? Переводные и оригинальные грамоты посольских книг Ивана III]
Anton Zimmerling
September 2024
 

This paper discusses the embassy books of Ivan III as sources for the sociolinguistic situation in the XV century Russian. The embassy books of Ivan III are a non-homogeneous collection of texts, where original letters written in the idiom of the Grand Duchy of Moscow were supplemented by the incoming letters written in other Slavic idioms and by the translated texts. I argue that the incoming letters written in other Late East Slavic idioms were normally not converted into the idiom of the Grand Duchy of Moscow. The microparametric variation in the idioms of the Grand Duchy and Moscow and the Grand Duchy of Lithuania can be described on the basis of the same parameters for word order and diagnostic constructions, such as the so-called vernacular plus perfect with BYL. Intralinguistic variation is occasionally displayed in translated letters written by bilingual scribes proficient in Tatar. Such scribes tended to focus on the speech of the younger Muscovites, while the authors of the original letters were more conservative. Two incoming 1501 letters from Kefe were written in an Early Ukrainian idiom. A clear marker of the Northern dialect was found in the translated document sent to Moscow from Ivangorod (Jaanilinn) in June 1505. The interpreter can be identified with the Moscow author Dmitry Gerasimov, who was of Novgorod origin.
