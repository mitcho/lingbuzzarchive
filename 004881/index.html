Rethinking (in)direct causation: two classes of lexical causative verbs
Josep Ausensi
December 2019
 

Traditionally, it is assumed that lexical causative verbs (e.g. kill, break, open) entail direct causation, whereas periphrastic causatives (e.g. cause to die/break/open) are compatible with indirect causation (Fodor 1970; Katz 1970; Wolff 2003; Rappaport Hovav and Levin 2012, i.a.). Roughly put, indirect causation involves an intermediate entity that intervenes between the causer and the final causee in a causal chain (Wolff 2003). Nonetheless, Neeleman and Van de Koot (2012) have recently argued that lexical causative verbs are indeed compatible with indirect causation. Similarly, Martin (2018) claims that lexical causative verbs do not entail direct causation, as direct causation is actually a defeasible inference. In this paper, I argue that lexical causative verbs do not constitute a uniform class and need to be split into two classes depending on whether they entail direct causation or not. As I argue, entailing direct causation is contingent on the semantics of the roots from which lexical causative verbs are derived.
