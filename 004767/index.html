Recursion in Humpback Whale Song
Malcolm Kennedy
March 2018
 

The most salient quality of humpback song is the repetition of subroutines within the larger routine that is the entire song — as well as the repetition of the song itself. Payne and McVay (1971, henceforth P&M) use this to posit a syntactic object smaller than the entire song but larger than its atomic sound units, called a phrase. P&M implicitly establish repeatability as a diagnostic of phrasehood: phrases are delineable from surrounding material because units internal to a phrase repeat to the exclusion of other units: if a song consists of two adjacent phrases [AB][CD], the sequences ABAB and CDCD will be attested, and crucially, BCBC will never be attested, since B and C do not form a phrase. This constituency test parallels those used in linguistic syntax, like co-ordination and pro-form replacement, and gives a means of falsifying posited constituents and proposing new ones. I extend this reasoning to phrase-internal structure in one humpback song type, and show that nested repetition phenomena in certain phrases cannot be explained without multiply-embedded, binary-branching hierarchical structures, with a depth of three phrasal layers in at least one case. I show that context-free grammars (CFGs), and not regular grammars, are able to generate the observed patterns without also overgenerating. I propose that humpbacks are the only known nonhuman species demonstrably capable of processing hierarchical structure at this depth. I explore what evolutionary insights might be provided by the existence of syntax in a potentially asemantic cognitive system like humpback song, particularly in light of proposals like Katz & Pesetsky (2009) that language and music might share a common mental syntax.
