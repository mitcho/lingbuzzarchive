The That-Trace Effect – a surface or a deep island phenomenon? Evidence from resumption and prolepsis in Igbo
Mary Amaechi, Doreen Georgi
October 2024
 

There are two major types of approaches to the subject / non-subject extraction asymmetry known as the that-trace effect: (a) structural accounts that prohibit subject extraction in the syntax; (b) surface-oriented PF accounts that rule out the surface string that results from long subject extraction. Pesetsky (2017) points out that it is still unclear what kind of phenomenon the that-trace effect is. In this paper, we argue that the effect has a syntactic cause in Igbo (Benue-Congo, Nigeria). The argument is based on a repair strategy for long subject A'-dependencies in Igbo that involves resumption. We show that even though Igbo in prinicple allows long A'-movement in the syntax and and also trace spell-out (= the ingredients for a widely pursued surface/PF-account of the that-trace effect), the resumption repair does not emerge from long subject movement, but rather involves prolepsis. We further argue - based on novel evidence involving subextraction from subjects in Igbo - that the syntactic restriction underlying the that-trace effect in Igbo is (Spec-to-Spec) antilocality. More generally, this paper studies the formation of long A'-dependencies in Igbo (including the description of extraction asymmetries), and shows that one (not surface-evident) option is prolepsis.
