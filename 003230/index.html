Case movement and theories of case
Karattuparambil Jayaseelan
March 2009
 

This paper examines the puzzle that the dative case apparently shows up in two positions in the Case Hierarchy: it can be on the complement of the locative case (and therefore be below the locative case in the hierarchy), call it DAT1; and it can take a locative-marked phrase as its complement (and therefore be above the locative case in the hierarchy), call it DAT2. There is a meaning difference that correlates with the positional difference: DAT1 is purely a structural case without any special meaning, while DAT2 has a directional meaning. The puzzle is solved by postulating case movement: the null head of a Path Phrase attracts the dative case to it. It is further shown that this movement interacts with the stranding-or-pied-piping options that generate word order differences as proposed in Jayaseelan (2010), to derive such facts as that Dutch signifies the locative/directional meaning difference by means of a preposition/postposition difference, and German signifies the same difference by a dative/accusative case alternation on the prepositional object.
